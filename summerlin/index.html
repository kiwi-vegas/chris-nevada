A Local's Perspective

Summerlin: Where Las Vegas Does Living Right

I've been working real estate in Las Vegas my entire career, and I watched Summerlin come up from bare desert back in 1990 when Howard Hughes Corporation broke ground on what would become the valley's most sought-after address. Named after Hughes' grandmother Jean Amelia Summerlin, this was never designed to be just another subdivision — it was designed to be a complete community, and thirty-five years later, that vision has held up remarkably well.

Positioned along the western rim of the Las Vegas Valley, Summerlin backs up directly against the Spring Mountains and Red Rock Canyon National Conservation Area. That backdrop changes everything. You're ten minutes from some of the most dramatic hiking, cycling, and rock climbing terrain in the country, while still being twenty minutes from the Strip. That combination is genuinely rare — most resort communities compromise one for the other. Summerlin doesn't.

What I've seen over the decades is that buyers who discover Summerlin rarely look anywhere else. The community is organized into 20-plus distinct villages — from the ultra-exclusive guard-gated estates of The Ridges to the family-friendly streets of The Paseos and Stonebridge. Each has its own feel and price point, all connected by 200-plus miles of trails that wind through parks, skirt golf courses, and lead right up into the natural desert arroyos. This wasn't infrastructure bolted on after the fact. It was baked into the blueprint from day one.

Through every market cycle I've worked — the booms, the corrections, the recoveries — Summerlin has consistently held its value better than almost anywhere else in Southern Nevada. The schools are excellent, the streets are clean, and the HOA actually enforces its standards. When someone tells me they want Las Vegas living without the chaos of Las Vegas, Summerlin is always my first call.

Outdoor Living

250 Parks, 200 Miles of Trails, One Extraordinary Backyard

People in Summerlin live outside. That's not marketing copy — it's what I've watched for thirty-five years. The trail system here is legitimate infrastructure, not decorative landscaping. It runs through every village, connects to natural desert arroyos, and eventually reaches Red Rock Canyon itself.

The planned expansion will push the trail network past 250 miles, including a dedicated 20-mile bike and hiking corridor from Stonebridge all the way to the Red Rock Visitor Center. That project alone has drawn serious attention from the cycling and outdoor community nationwide.

Common Questions

Summerlin Home Buyer FAQ

Thirty-five years of answering these questions — here are the ones that come up every time.

Summerlin has a two-layer HOA structure. There's a master association (one of four — North, South, West, or Centre) that runs about $55–$65 per month, and a neighborhood-level HOA that typically adds another $50–$150 depending on the community. For a typical single-family home you're looking at $100–$200 total per month. What you get in return is genuinely well-maintained infrastructure, enforced architectural standards, and 250+ parks and trails that make the community worth the fee. Guard-gated communities like The Ridges and Red Rock Country Club carry higher fees that also cover security staffing and private amenities.
The Paseos and Stonebridge consistently come up as top choices for families. Both are close to Fox Hill Park — one of the best adventure playgrounds in the valley — and are zoned for some of Summerlin's stronger public schools. If private school is a priority, any village puts you within a short drive of Bishop Gorman, Faith Lutheran, or The Meadows. One important note: school assignments within CCSD are zip-code and address specific, so always confirm the assigned school for any specific property before you buy. I run these checks as a standard part of our buyer process.
Most Summerlin addresses are 12–15 miles from the Strip — about 20 minutes in normal traffic via Summerlin Parkway to I-15. During major conventions or peak Strip traffic, expect 25–30 minutes. Going the other direction, Red Rock Canyon is 10–15 minutes from most neighborhoods. That's the deal you're making in Summerlin, and the vast majority of residents consider it a very good deal.
Yes, several. The Ridges is the most well-known — 793 acres with staffed gates and some of the most impressive custom construction in Las Vegas. Red Rock Country Club is a private, gated golf community with two Arnold Palmer-designed courses. The Summit Club is the valley's most exclusive gated enclave, with a Tom Fazio course and invitation-only residency. There are also many smaller gated communities throughout Summerlin at more accessible price points. I know most of them personally — just tell me your priorities and I'll narrow it down.
Summerlin covers a genuinely wide range. You can find condos and townhomes starting around $400K–$450K, which opens the door for first-time buyers. Single-family homes in established villages like The Canyons or The Trails typically start in the $550K–$700K range. Move up into The Paseos or Stonebridge and you're looking at $650K–$1.2M. The Ridges runs $1.5M to well over $5M for custom estates. The Summit Club starts around $5M and goes up from there. Current median for all of Summerlin is roughly $686,000, though that varies significantly by village and sub-area.
Based on thirty-five years of watching this market, Summerlin has one of the strongest track records in Southern Nevada for value retention and appreciation. It has ranked among the nation's top-selling master-planned communities for over three decades — that kind of sustained demand reflects real underlying value. In 2025, Summerlin South saw nearly 5.5% year-over-year appreciation. The community still has approximately 5,000 acres of planned future development, which should support continued demand. No investment is guaranteed, but if you're going to own real estate in Las Vegas, Summerlin is historically a very sound choice.
Downtown Summerlin is a 400-acre outdoor lifestyle center with 125+ shops and restaurants — anchored by Nordstrom Rack, Macy's, Dillard's, a large Whole Foods, and a PGA Tour Superstore. The dining is legitimately good: True Food Kitchen, Harlo Steakhouse, Lazy Dog, Maggiano's, and plenty more. Las Vegas Ballpark is there (home of the Aviators, the A's Triple-A affiliate), and City National Arena is where the Golden Knights hold their practice sessions. It's a walkable, outdoor lifestyle center — something Las Vegas doesn't have much of, which is part of what makes Summerlin feel distinctly different from the rest of the valley.
